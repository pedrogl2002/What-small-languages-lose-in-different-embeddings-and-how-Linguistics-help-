In this essay, I explored a question that I think is becoming increasingly important in NLP and multilingual AI:
What happens when low-resource languages are represented through embedding spaces shaped mainly by dominant languages such as English?
Much of the current research on low-resource NLP focuses on solving the problem through technical methods: synthetic data generation, few-shot prompting, transfer learning and efficient model adaptation. These approaches are necessary, but my essay argues that they are not enough on their own.
The central idea is that multilingual models may preserve the general meaning of a sentence while still weakening important language-specific distinctions. These can include politeness systems, subject omission, evidentiality, alienable and inalienable possession, inclusive/exclusive pronouns, and other features that carry cultural, pragmatic or social meaning.
Using examples from languages such as Japanese, Spanish, Tongan and Turkish, I discuss how meaning can be compressed when a language is mapped into a semantic space shaped by another language. The problem is not simply translation loss, but representational integrity: whether language models preserve the categories and distinctions that matter inside each language.
One of the main conclusions of the essay is that linguists should be considered essential contributors to low-resource NLP. They can help identify what is being lost, guide corpus design and annotation, and create evaluations that go beyond surface-level similarity.
Multilingual AI should not only aim to support more languages. It should aim to represent them on their own terms.
